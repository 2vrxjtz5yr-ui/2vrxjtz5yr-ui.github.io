Traditional Semolina Gnocchi

Servings: 4 to 6

Source: Mastering Pasta (Page 219)

Also known as gnocchi alla romana, these little gnocchi originated in Rome but have become popular all over Italy. Instead of shaping the dough into dumplings, you punch out rounds and bake them with butter and Parmesan. This is the basic, traditional recipe, but feel free to flavor it like pasta dough, using whatever herbs, spices, or seasonings you like. Add chopped, cooked spinach or other cooked vegetables. Have some fun with it! You'll end up with a bunch of scraps, and you can bake them in another dish if you like. Or, cut the dough into squares so that you don't end up with any scraps. You can also just spread the gnocchi dough in the bottom of a large baking dish right from the start.

Ingredients

  • 4½ cups (1 L) whole milk
  • 1½ cups (250 g) semolina
  • 1 cup (100 g) grated Parmesan cheese
  • 7 tablespoons (100 g) unsalted butter, plus some for greasing
  • Kosher salt and freshly ground black pepper
  • 3 egg yolks

Instructions

  1. Lay a large sheet of aluminum foil on a flat work surface and grease it with butter.
  2. Pour the milk into a saucepan and bring it to a boil over medium heat. Gradually add the semolina while whisking constantly to prevent lumps. Cook, whisking, until slightly thickened, about 30 seconds. Remove from the heat and quickly stir in ½ cup (50 g) of the Parmesan and 3½ tablespoons (50 g) of the butter. Taste the mixture, adding salt and pepper until it tastes good to you. Quickly stir in the egg yolks before the mixture begins to set up.
  3. Pour the mixture onto the prepared aluminum foil and gently pat it with your hands or roll it with a rolling pin until it is about an even ½ inch (12 mm) thick. Let the dough cool until set, about 30 minutes or up to 1 hour.
  4. Heat the oven to 400°F (200°C). Butter a shallow 4-quart (3.8 L) baking dish. Using a round cutter 2 to 3 inches (5 to 7.5 cm) in diameter, punch out circles of dough. Arrange the cutouts in a single layer in the prepared baking dish without leaving too much space between the rounds.
  5. Melt the remaining 3½ tablespoons (50 g) butter in a small saucepan over medium heat. Pour the melted butter evenly over the gnocchi, and then top them evenly with the remaining ½ cup (50 g) Parmesan. Bake until the Parmesan is lightly browned and the gnocchi are piping hot, about 20 minutes.
  6. Dish out the gnocchi onto warmed plates.
