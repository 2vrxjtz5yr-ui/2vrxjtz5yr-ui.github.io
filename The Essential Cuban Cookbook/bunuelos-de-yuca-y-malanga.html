Buñuelos de Yuca y Malanga / Yucca and Malanga Fritters

Serves 8 | Prep Time: 20 minutes | Cook Time: 45 minutes

Would you believe that up until developing this cookbook, I had never made buñuelos? These light, airy fritters are a cross between a doughnut and a beignet. The Cuban version is usually made with yucca or a yucca/malanga (a root vegetable, commonly used in Cuban cuisine) pastry dough, giving them a delicious starchy texture. They are twisted, fried, shaped into a number 8 (which signifies good luck), and served with a sweet syrup or powdered sugar. I now regret not paying attention when my mom made these every year during the holidays. This version is adapted from a recipe by the famed Cuban chef, Nitza Villapol.

Variation Tip: Malanga (fresh or frozen) and sweet potatoes can also be used to make buñuelos in addition to the yucca, or as a replacement. Use equal parts of each vegetable to total 2 pounds.

Ingredients

  • 2 teaspoons kosher salt, divided
  • 2 pounds yucca, peeled and cut into 1-inch chunks
  • 2 cups sugar
  • 1 cup water
  • 1 teaspoon grated lemon zest
  • 3 cinnamon sticks
  • 3 star anise
  • 1 large egg
  • 1 teaspoon ground aniseed
  • ½ cup all-purpose flour, plus more for dusting
  • 2 cups canola oil

Instructions

  1. In a large Dutch oven or soup pot, combine 1 teaspoon of salt, the yucca, and enough water to cover. Bring to a boil over medium-high heat and immediately reduce the heat to medium-low. Cover the pot and simmer for 20 to 25 minutes until the yucca is fork-tender. Drain and press the yucca through a food mill or ricer, or mash with a potato masher. Spread the mixture evenly in a baking pan to cool at room temperature.
  2. Meanwhile, in a small saucepan, combine the sugar, water, and lemon zest. Bring to a boil and cook for 3 to 4 minutes until the sugar dissolves. Stir in the cinnamon sticks and star anise. Remove from the heat and set aside for at least 30 minutes for the flavors to develop.
  3. In a large bowl, whisk the egg, ground aniseed, and remaining 1 teaspoon of salt. Add the mashed vegetable mixture and whisk to combine, slowly adding the flour to form a smooth but slightly sticky dough.
  4. Dust a clean work surface with flour and transfer the dough to it. Roll the dough into a ½-inch-thick rope, then divide the rope into 6-inch pieces. Lightly dust the tops with flour. Shape each piece into a figure 8, adding flour to the work surface, as needed, to keep the dough from sticking.
  5. In a large Dutch oven or heavy-bottom pan, heat the oil to 375°F. Carefully add the buñuelos to the hot oil, working in batches of 3 or 4 pieces at a time. Deep fry for 3 to 4 minutes until golden, turning occasionally. Transfer the fried buñuelos to a wire cooling rack. Drizzle with the syrup and serve warm.

Source: Recipe from The Essential Cuban Cookbook
